Women’s Participation in Global Climate Conferences

Jenna Graff, Gwynnie Hayes, & Aria Whalen

Introduction

As the effects of climate change are felt increasingly across countries, exploring how effective environmental policy is created and enforced is becoming imperative to create a sustainable world. Given the global nature of climate change, we are interested in how international politics plays a role in decision making. One yearly conference held by the members of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is the Convention of the Parties (COP). These international meetings have been held yearly since 1995 (sans 2020) and offer a case study to understand how women have and continue to influence global environmental politics.

The makeup of actors in these conventions has primarily been older white men, which influences policy outcomes (Uhre, 2014). Studies have found conflicting evidence on a relationship between gender and the environment at a political level; while some find that female legislators are more concerned about environmental issues when compared to men, others find that after accounting for political party, gender is insignificant (Baraldi & Fosco, 2025). Regardless, we believe that enough research has found a correlation between female legislators and environmental policies to merit examination of patterns in gender diversity in global environmental politics. Policies aimed to rectify inequality at an international level cannot be effective until we understand the factors that create it. Therefore, we hope to examine factors that may predict the proportion of women involved in the COPs for each nation over time.

Specifically, we are curious about how the attendance of women at COP meetings has changed over the 30 years that the convention has taken place. Much of this work is based on a similar examination by Andreas Uhre on the diversity of transnational actors across Global Environmental Summits. Uhre found that important predictors included financial resources and strength of democracy. On a national scale, factors we believe are important to examine include national measures of wealth, human rights, and political freedoms. We hypothesize that after accounting for a general increase in proportion of women participation in COPs over time, there will be predictive power in global indicators to explain differences in women participation among countries.

Materials & Methods

To conduct this analysis, we brought together 5 datasets detailing Country Level statistics on COP meeting attendance from 1995-2024, Human Rights Index, Democracy Index, GDP per Capita, and Regional Location Data. To explore our questions we used these predictors to create a multilevel binomial model to predict the odds of a country’s COP delegate being a woman.
The basis of our project is the COP meeting attendance data from the UNFCCC data, which was compiled into a dataset by Daria Blinova, Rakesh Emuru & Benjamin E. Bagozzi for their article on “Individual attendance data for over 30 years of international climate change talks”. Using text extraction and OCR tools the researchers combed through thousands of pdf attendance rosters from each COP meeting to compile a dataset of the delegates party, name, honorific, job, and association, as well as the year of the COP meeting and where it was held. From this information, they created variables for the gender of the delegate based on honorific, and several other binary variables for the meeting type and delegation party (Blinova et al., 2024). To explore our questions, we filtered the data to only include governmental delegations, grouped the data by country and year, and counted by the number of delegates, so that we would ultimately end up with one row per country for each year they attended, and a total number of delegates that country sent that year. We totalled the number of female delegates per delegation, and divided that by the previous total to get a proportion of female delegates per country per year, our response variable.

 Based on background research, we chose to look at three country level global indicators for our predictors. First, we joined GDP per Capita data from the World Bank as a level 2 predictor; these values didn’t change meaningfully among countries from 1995 to 2024, so the latest available data was joined as a constant variable between years. We then joined the Human Rights Index (HRI) from V-dem via Our World in Data as a level 2 predictor, for the same reason as above. To do this, we sliced the dataset to only include the most recent available data for each country, and joined that as a constant variable between years in our COP dataset. The value of the (HRI) ranges from 0 to 1 and is defined as, “the extent to which people are free from government torture, political killings, and forced labor, have property rights, and enjoy the freedoms of movement, religion, expression, and association” (V-Dem (2025) – processed by Our World in Data). Lastly, Democracy Index (DI) data was joined from the Economist Intelligence Unit via Our World in Data as a level 1 predictor that changes from year to year. The value of the DI ranges from 0 to 10 and is defined as, “extent to which citizens can choose their political leaders in free and fair elections, enjoy civil liberties, prefer democracy over other political systems, can and do participate in politics, and have a functioning government that acts on their behalf” (Economist Intelligence Unit (2006-2024) – processed by Our World in Data). It is also important to note that DI data was only available for certain years, resulting in a large quantity of missing data; the model should adjust for this automatically. Lastly, we joined regional data to have a continent level predictor.

To clean our data, we used tools to webscrape, filter, mutate, pivot, and extensively rename/recode variables to unify the full dataset we have now. NA’s in gender were dropped from the dataset. Furthermore, the variable for meeting year was centered on 1995 for meaningful analysis, and GDP per capita was logged to make comparisons meaningful. We also separated the original combined continent “Americas” into Northern America (USA & Canada) and Latin America & Caribbean (From Mexico through South America) for more specific comparisons.
We chose to use a multilevel model to answer our research question as the data is nested by years in countries—analyzing it this way helps to control for the implicit bias caused by these nested units. Though an argument could be made that our data is also nested by continent, we chose to just use a two level approach as we have no continent level variables, and we are just interested in the impact of fixed effects from continents. We chose to specifically use a binomial multilevel model as our response variable is a proportion of an interest group divided by a total with multiple predictors. The model was weighted by the total number of delegates that attended. All analysis was done in R, and our model was run using the glmer function in lme4. Due to the complexity of our model, we just calculated ui for our random effects; the interpretation of this is that the effect of time is uniform for all countries.

To begin our modeling process, we looked at summary statistics for numeric variables. Table 1 shows the summary of the relevant numeric variables in our final model, excluding Meeting Year as it stayed relatively constant throughout the dataset. There is a large variation in the number of delegates from each country, which is why we used it as a weight later on in the modeling process. DI data is not recorded for every year, resulting in a high number of NA’s.

Fig. 1 shows four important relationships. First, there is a correlation between a country’s DI Score and the proportion of women in their delegation; there is a decent amount of variability from the line, but a clear trend exists. Over time, we see there is an increasing average proportion of women in COP delegations.

There may also be some potential explanatory value of a country’s HRI and log(GDP per Capita) on the proportion of women involved in a COP summit, though there is a much higher amount of variability. Log(GDP per capita) is especially variable.

From Fig. 2, we can see that Northern America has the highest intercept for the proportion of women in a country’s delegation. However, the slope seems to be slightly flatter than many of the other continents; Europe, Latin America & The Caribbean, and Oceania are at a very similar proportion of women in the most recent meeting years despite having a much lower intercept. Africa and Asia have lower intercepts, as well as a slope comparable to Northern America.

An unconditional growth model showed that meeting year is significant in predicting the proportion of women involved in COP delegations. This is unsurprising given the steady increase in proportion of women by meeting year shown in Figure 1. All subsequent models included our variables of interest: GDP per capita, HRI, and DI. Interactions between DI and level 2 variables were tested, but removed from the final model due to insignificance. Our final model is as follows:

Level 1:

\[logodds_{ij} = a_i + b_i(Meeting Year_{ij}) + c_i(DI_{ij})\]

Level 2:

\[a_i = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1(GDPperCapita_i) + \alpha_2(Continent_i) + \alpha_3(HRI_i) + u_i\] \[b_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1(GDPperCapita_i) + \beta_2(Continent_i) + \beta_3(HRI_i)\] \[c_i = \gamma_0\]

Composite Model:

\[logodds_{ij}= \alpha_0 + \alpha_1(GDPperCapita_i) + \alpha_2(Continent_i) + \alpha_3(HRI_i) + u_i + \] \[\beta_0(MeetingYear_{ij}) + \beta_1(GDPperCapita_i:MeetingYear_{ij}) + \] \[\beta_2(HRI_i:MeetingYear_{ij}) + \beta_3(Continent_i:MeetingYear_{ij} + \gamma_0(DI_{ij})\]

\[Where\ u_i\sim N(0, {\sigma_u}^2) \]

A real world interpretation of Meeting Year × Europe would not have GDP per capita or HRI at 0. A quick summary of our data reveals an average Log GDP per Capita and HRI in Europe are 10.4 and 0.866 respectively. Discussion

Our model shows clear evidence that a variety of Global Indicators are indeed influential in the proportion of women that enter into global environmental politics at COP meetings. There is a steady and significant increase to the proportion of women attending COP meetings. Furthermore, we found that increases in the Human Rights Index and Democracy Index are both significantly correlated with an increased probability of women joining a delegation at COP meetings. These findings are in line with past studies (Uhre, 2014). However, in our final model we did not find GDP per Capita to be a significant variable on its own, though it was when interacting with the meeting year. This is starkly in contrast to Uhre’s other finding, which was that financial resources are significantly correlated with the diversity of participation in Trans-National Actors at Global Environmental Political Meetings. Notably, GDP per Capita was significant before adding in continent level predictors, signalling that GDP per Capita is likely still significantly correlated with the proportion of women but is being overexplained by Continents.

The most common significant interaction was on meeting year, with all predictors besides DI interacting significantly, and being included in the final model. While Continent × Meeting Year are easily interpreted as above, that is not truly representative of the whole story. As mentioned in the results, setting GDP per Capita and HRI to 0 to interpret a model is not realistic. By adjusting the model to include continental averages we see that Europe’s effect on proportion over time shifts from flat to negative. This difference reveals that real world applications of this data are highly complex and should include a more nuanced interpretation.

These results show a clear trend that an increase in governmental resources, democracy, and human rights are impactful on the proportion of women involved in their country’s environmental politics. These findings are crucial to overall equality, but this shift towards more feminine politics also may also have impacts on sustainable measures that come out of these meetings (Baraldi & Fosco, 2025). Based on this model, countries that wish to improve their gender equality in environmental politics (specifically at COP meetings) should put an increased level of effort into their fairness of democracy and the human rights of their citizens.

The strength of our analysis is that it builds upon work from past papers to connect COP data to global indicators and paint a new more full image of what factors lead to increased diversity in a section of global environmental politics. The weakness of our analysis is in the complexity of our model. Due to the size and complexity of our model, we could not explore random effects on a meaningful level and our model repeatedly failed to reach convergence. Possible confounding variables in our study might include individual government gender equality statistics or other global indicators we didn’t include in our study. Future studies should test the impact of a meeting being in the same region as the delegation’s country, as well as other indicators such as birth rates or rates of women’s education.
